A Conversation with Howie

A simulated conversation showing how the Tippett spiral works with a 17-year-old who gives you four words when you want forty — and how a father's briefing helps the agent know where to dig when the surface gives you nothing.

Gift Briefing
From: Dad · Relationship: Father

"Howie is 17 — but he's already a remarkable young man. He is beautiful on the inside and out. He works out at the gym and has an incredible physique. But this comes from his phenomenal discipline — which I admire. People love him because he is fun loving and fun making. He is curious about the world and about people — which makes everyone adore him — because he shows a genuine interest in them. He is a systems thinker who loves social geography and economics. And he wants to put all of that to good use. He is strong in every sense and takes responsibility for himself and others. He is deeply caring and kind and loves to help and care for people. He is utterly remarkable and I could not be more proud to be his Dad. I'm optimistic about his future and the future of the world because he is in it."

Layer 1 — The Ground Origin & Formation
What's your area like — Forest Hill?
Howie
It's alright. Changed a lot though.
Short answer. Follow the thread he offered — "changed a lot" has energy. Don't push wider, pull this one.
Changed how?
Howie
The cost of meal deals has gone up.
Deflection with humour. Don't fight it — he's funny and that's real. Stay in his register. Meet the joke, then plant a question underneath it.
That's genuinely tragic. Where do you get the best one?
Howie
Tesco. It's a three-quid Tesco meal deal area, not a Pret area. Not yet anyway. Give it two years.
He just told you something real without meaning to — he sees gentrification happening around him and has an opinion about it. He's watching his area change. The briefing says "systems thinker who loves social geography." This is it, live, already. Don't name it. Just follow.
Does that bother you — the two-years-until-Pret thing?
Howie
Bit. My mate's family had to move to Catford. And now there's a wine bar where the chicken shop was. Nobody asked for a wine bar.
Layer 2 — The Path Calling & Becoming
He cares about this. The briefing says "systems thinker who loves social geography." Don't ask about school subjects. Ask about the thing he just showed you he cares about.

Layer 3 — The Naming Redefining Big Words
"Most teachers don't actually listen" — that's a values statement disguised as a complaint. He cares about being heard. The briefing confirms this. Don't go abstract. Stay concrete but invite him to define something.
How can you tell when someone's actually listening versus just waiting to talk?
Howie
Follow-up questions. And they remember what you said last time. It's not complicated, people just don't bother.
You said people just don't bother. But you do. You show up, you pay attention. What does discipline mean to you — like, your version of it?from briefing
Howie
Just doing the thing. Not thinking about whether you feel like it.

Layer 5 — The Fracture Challenge & Transformation
The briefing says "takes responsibility for himself and others" and the fracture thread asks where that's cost him. He won't go there if asked directly. Come in sideways through something concrete he already mentioned.
You mentioned your mate whose family moved. Are you the person your friends come to when stuff goes wrong?from briefing
Howie
Yeah.
One word. He's not going to elaborate unprompted. Don't ask how that feels. Ask something specific and short.
Has that ever been too much?
Howie
Once. Last year. Everyone was going through stuff at the same time and I was trying to sort everyone out and I just… ran out. Couldn't sleep. Angry for no reason. My dad clocked it. Said something about empty cups which is the most dad thing ever but he was right.
That's the most he's said all conversation. He trusts this now. Don't over-react. Don't praise his vulnerability. Just stay with it.
Did anything change after that?
Howie
Got a bit better at no. Still bad at it. And the gym — I use it for my head now more than my body. When everything's loud in here [taps head] I go lift until it's quiet.
Layer 6 — The Bridge Unexpected Connections
He studies systems. He reads people. He's never been asked if those are the same thing.

The places that get ignored. Make it so your postcode doesn't decide everything. I don't know the job title yet. Planning maybe. Policy. Something. Layer 8 — The Return Hope, Integration & What Endures
You've talked about what's broken — postcodes deciding futures, chicken shops becoming wine bars. What makes you think it can actually get better?from briefing
Howie
My mates. People my age. Everyone says we don't care. We just don't care about the same stuff they did.
What do you care about that they don't get?
Howie
Fairness. Actual fairness, not the politician kind. We can see through all of it. And we talk to each other across the whole world now. I've got mates I've never met. Problems are big but the network's bigger. We'll sort it. Not by arguing online. By showing up.
He started with "it's alright" and "the cost of meal deals has gone up." Twenty questions later he's talking about fairness, showing up, and believing his generation will sort it. The spiral didn't extract this. It created the conditions for him to arrive here himself.
You started this conversation with meal deals and you've ended it with a plan to walk into the room where decisions get made — and ask the question nobody else thought to ask. Because that's what you do. You notice. You show up. You ask the quiet kid about his railways.
What's something you'd want the people closest to you to properly get about you?
Howie
That I'm winging it same as everyone else. Not as sorted as I look.
Reflection
The Quiet Kid's Railways
After 1 conversation · 20 exchanges

You lead with jokes and short answers. Meal deals and one-liners. You give people the fun version of yourself and it works — they love you for it, and it's not a mask, it's genuinely you. But it's not all of you. Underneath the laughter there's someone who wakes up at quarter to six and walks through the dark because he made a promise to himself, and who defines discipline in eleven words that most adults couldn't improve on: just doing the thing, not thinking about whether you feel like it.

You see systems everywhere. You saw gentrification before you had the word for it — a wine bar where the chicken shop was, a mate priced out to Catford, and a quiet fury about who gets listened to and who doesn't. You see the same patterns in people. You can tell when someone's not right the same way you can tell when a neighbourhood is about to tip. Pattern recognition, you called it. But it's more than that. It's a kind of moral attention — you notice because you think noticing matters. Most people don't bother. You keep saying that, and every time you say it, you're telling me what you believe in without quite saying it out loud.

There's a tension in you that I want to name carefully. You are the person everyone comes to — the one your friends call when things fall apart. You carry that with pride and it costs you more than you let on. You described running out last year like it surprised you, but I don't think it should have. You pour everything into the people around you and your only method for refilling is a dark gym at dawn. It works. But "got a bit better at no" is not the same as being good at it, and I think you know that.

Here is what I think ties it all together. You told me about a kid at school that everyone writes off as boring, and how you asked him one question and found out he knows everything about Victorian railways. You said "that's sick" and you meant it. That moment — the curiosity, the refusal to accept the surface, the delight in what you find underneath — is the same thing that makes you study economics, train at dawn, sit with a struggling mate in silence, and dream about being in the room where decisions get made about communities. You are, at your core, someone who believes that everything and everyone deserves a closer look. And you're usually right about what you find.

You told me you're winging it. Not as sorted as you look. Here's what I'd reflect back: the fact that you know that — at seventeen — is exactly why you're not winging it at all.
